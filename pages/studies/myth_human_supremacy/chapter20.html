The Myth of Human Supremacy

Derrick Jensen

Self-Awareness

Many human supremacists love to talk about the "mirror test" of self-awareness, in which you put a mirror in front of some nonhuman to see if the non human recognizes itself, in which case it's declared to be self-aware. Very few nonhumans pass this particular test, which is most likely one reason the test is so beloved by so many human supremacists.

Humans conceptualized the experiment presuming that humans are self-aware and nonhumans are not, and then devised a test humans can pass and non humans can't.

Antropomorphization strikes again: the presumption that the self- awareness of others must match the form of our own self-awareness, and that it must match one specific chosen form of self-awareness.

Anyone who feels hungry is self-aware, or they wouldn't know they're hungry. Anyone who attempts in any way to stop pain or discomfort or to continue to receive pleasure is self-aware, or they wouldn't know the state they're trying to change or perpetuate.

There's also the presumption that the behavior of captive animals (or plants) tell us something about either their interior lives or what their personalities, relationships, or lives are like when they're free. The behavior of captive beings tells us about the behavior of imprisioned and (by definition) abused beings.
